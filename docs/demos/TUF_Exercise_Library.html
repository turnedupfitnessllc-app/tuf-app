MOVEMENT PATTERN 01 · UPPER BODY
PUSH
Pushing movements train the chest, shoulders, and triceps — the primary muscles responsible for pressing objects away from your body. After 40, these muscles support posture correction and help counterbalance the forward-rounding that comes from years of desk work and driving.

40+ SCIENCE — WHY PUSH MATTERS
After 40, the rotator cuff tendons begin losing tensile strength at roughly 1% per year. Controlled pushing movements — particularly those that keep the elbows at 45 degrees rather than flared — actively load these tendons within their safe range, stimulating collagen production and slowing that decline. Push training also increases bone density in the wrist, forearm, and shoulder girdle through compressive loading — one of the most effective natural defenses against osteoporosis.
💪 PUSH EXERCISES — BEGINNER TO ADVANCED
01
Wall Push-Up
BEGINNER
NO EQUIPMENTSHOULDER SAFEKNEE SAFE
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Stand arm's length from a wall. Place hands flat on the wall at shoulder width and shoulder height.
2
Keep your body in a straight line from head to heels — engage your core before moving.
3
Bend elbows at 45 degrees and lower your chest toward the wall with control. Don't let hips sag forward.
4
Press back to start. Full extension without locking elbows. That's one rep.
3 SETS10–15 REPSREST 45 SEC
MODIFICATIONS
SHOULDER ISSUES
Narrow your hand position to just inside shoulder width. This reduces rotator cuff stress while maintaining chest activation.
LOWER BACK PAIN
This is one of the safest options. Ensure full core engagement and avoid arching through the lower back at the top.
PROGRESSION CUE
When 3×15 feels easy with perfect form, move to the Incline Push-Up using a countertop or bench.
02
Incline Push-Up
BEGINNER+
BENCH/COUNTERCHEST FOCUSSHOULDER SAFE
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Place hands on a bench, counter, or sturdy chair. Body forms a diagonal line — the lower the surface, the harder the movement.
2
Set feet together or hip-width. Brace your core and squeeze your glutes — your body should move as one unit.
3
Lower your chest to the surface, elbows tracking at 45 degrees from your torso. Chin stays neutral — don't lead with your face.
4
Press through your palms to full extension. Exhale on the press.
3 SETS8–12 REPSREST 60 SEC
MODIFICATIONS
SHOULDER ISSUES
Use a slightly wider grip and higher surface angle. If you feel pinching at the top of the movement, raise the surface height 2–3 inches.
WRIST PAIN
Make fists and perform on knuckles, or use push-up handles if available. This keeps the wrist in a neutral position.
PROGRESSION CUE
Lower the surface height progressively — kitchen counter → dining chair → coffee table → floor. Each drop increases difficulty by roughly 15%.
03
Knee Push-Up
BEGINNER
NO EQUIPMENTFLOORFULL RANGE
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Start on all fours. Walk hands forward until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders. Cross your feet behind you.
2
Hands slightly wider than shoulder width. Keep hips in line — the most common mistake is allowing the hips to pike up.
3
Lower chest to just above the floor. Pause for one count at the bottom. Don't rest on the floor.
4
Press back to start with full elbow extension. That's one rep.
3 SETS8–10 REPSREST 60 SEC
MODIFICATIONS
KNEE DISCOMFORT
Fold a yoga mat or towel under your knees to add padding. Some people also prefer to keep feet on the floor rather than crossed.
SHOULDER ISSUES
Move to a partial range of motion — lower only halfway until shoulder mobility improves.
PROGRESSION CUE
Target: 3 sets of 12 with a 2-second descent. Once achieved, begin transitioning to full floor push-up by placing toes down and lifting knees slightly.
04
Full Push-Up
INTERMEDIATE
NO EQUIPMENTFULL BODYBENCHMARK
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
High plank position. Hands shoulder-width, fingers spread, weight distributed across the entire hand — not just the heel of the palm.
2
Full body tension — glutes tight, core braced, thighs squeezed. Your body moves as one solid unit.
3
Descend with control — 2 to 3 seconds down. Chest brushes the floor. Elbows at 45 degrees, not flared wide.
4
Explosive drive back to top. Fully lock out arms. Reset tension and repeat.
3 SETS5–10 REPSREST 75 SEC
MODIFICATIONS
LOWER BACK PAIN
Focus intensely on core bracing before every rep. If the lower back sags before rep 5, drop to knee push-ups for remaining sets.
SHOULDER ISSUES
Use a slightly narrower grip (hands directly under shoulders). Avoid locking out completely at the top if you feel impingement.
PROGRESSION CUE
The 40+ standard of fitness: 3×10 with full range, controlled descent, zero hip sag. Achieve this and you're ready for loaded variations.

1
Start in a downward dog position — hips high, forming an inverted V. Hands shoulder-width, fingers spread.
2
Keep hips elevated throughout. Lower the top of your head toward the floor between your hands.

PROGRESSION CUE
This is your bridge to overhead pressing with weights. When 3×8 is controlled, you're ready for dumbbell shoulder press.

MOVEMENT PATTERN 02 · UPPER BODY
PULL
Pulling movements are the single most neglected pattern in beginner fitness. After 40, years of forward posture — phones, computers, steering wheels — create chronic tightness in the chest and weakness in the upper back. Pulling directly reverses this. Every rep is corrective therapy.
UPPER BACKREAR DELTOIDSBICEPSRHOMBOIDSROTATOR CUFF

🔬
40+ SCIENCE — WHY PULL MATTERS
The upper back muscles — particularly the rhomboids and lower trapezius — are chronically underactive in adults over 40 due to sedentary posture patterns. Weakness in these muscles allows the shoulders to round forward, placing the cervical spine in a compromised position that contributes to neck pain, headaches, and rotator cuff impingement. Just 2 pulling sessions per week has been shown to measurably reduce forward head posture within 8 weeks.
💪 PULL EXERCISES — BEGINNER TO ADVANCED
01
Door Frame Row
BEGINNER
NO EQUIPMENTHOME FRIENDLY
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Stand in a doorway and grip both sides of the frame at hip height. Walk feet forward until your body leans back at roughly 45 degrees.
2
Arms fully extended. Body straight from ears to heels. This is your starting position.
3
Pull your chest toward the door frame. Lead with your elbows — drive them back and down. Squeeze your shoulder blades together at the top.
4
Lower with control — a 3-second descent is the goal. Feel the stretch across your upper back.
3 SETS10–15 REPSREST 60 SEC
MODIFICATIONS
SHOULDER ISSUES
Use a neutral grip (palms facing each other) if possible. Keep elbows tucked closer to your sides rather than flaring outward.
LOWER BACK PAIN
Walk feet closer to the door to reduce the angle and body weight being lifted. Less angle = less load.
PROGRESSION CUE
Walk feet further forward to increase the challenge. As you get parallel to the floor, you're doing a true bodyweight row.
02
Resistance Band Row
BEGINNER
RESISTANCE BANDSEATEDADJUSTABLE
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Sit on the floor with legs extended. Loop a resistance band around the bottom of your feet. Hold one end in each hand.
2
Sit tall — chest up, slight natural curve in the lower back. Don't slouch. Think: "proud chest."
3
Pull the band toward your lower ribcage. Elbows travel close to your sides. Squeeze shoulder blades together at the end of the movement.
4
Slowly return to start — resist the band on the way out. This is where upper back muscles work hardest.
3 SETS12–15 REPSREST 60 SEC
MODIFICATIONS
LOWER BACK PAIN
Bend knees slightly to reduce hamstring tension which can cause you to round your lower back. Or perform in a chair — anchor the band to a door handle at chest height.
SHOULDER ISSUES
Keep elbows at 90 degrees rather than pulling all the way back. Focus on the mid-back squeeze rather than full elbow extension behind the body.
PROGRESSION CUE
Use a thicker resistance band or double-loop for more challenge. When 3×15 feels easy, move to a standing single-arm dumbbell row.
03
Band Pull-Apart
BEGINNER
RESISTANCE BANDPOSTURE FIXSHOULDER HEALTH
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Hold a light resistance band with both hands, arms extended in front at shoulder height. Palms down, hands shoulder-width apart.
2
Keep arms straight. Pull the band apart by moving both hands outward to your sides — like opening a curtain.
3
The band should reach your chest. Hold for one count and actively squeeze your shoulder blades together. This is the most important part.
4
Slowly return to start. Resist the band — don't let it snap back.
3 SETS15–20 REPSREST 45 SEC
SHOULDER ISSUES
This exercise is often prescribed specifically for shoulder rehabilitation. If you feel pain, reduce band resistance significantly — a very light band is sufficient to activate the rear deltoids.
PROGRESSION CUE
This is a "forever exercise" — it should be done as a warm-up for every upper body session regardless of fitness level. Progress by adding reps, not resistance.

HOW TO PERFORM
1
Lie under a sturdy table. Grip the edge with both hands, shoulder-width. Your chest should be directly below the table edge.
2
Extend legs so you're in a reverse plank — only your heels on the floor, body straight as a board.

Pull your chest up to the table edge. Lead with your chest, not your chin. Elbows drive back and down.

Elevate feet on a chair to increase difficulty. When 3×10 with feet elevated is solid, you're ready for assisted pull-ups.
05
Single-Arm Dumbbell Row
INTERMEDIATE
DUMBBELLUNILATERALLOADED
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Place one knee and same-side hand on a bench or chair. Opposite foot flat on the floor. Back is flat and parallel to the ground.
2
Hold dumbbell in free hand, arm hanging straight. Don't rotate the shoulder — keep it packed.
3
Drive the elbow up and back — think "starting a lawn mower." The dumbbell travels in a straight vertical line. No swinging.
4
Hold at the top for one count — feel the full contraction in the lat. Lower with full control.

SHOULDER ISSUES
Stop the row when your elbow is at 90 degrees rather than pulling past your torso. This partial range still builds back strength without impingement.
PROGRESSION CUE
Increase weight in 2.5 lb increments when 3×10 is clean and controlled. This is the most scalable pulling exercise available at home.

MOVEMENT PATTERN 03 · LOWER BODY
SQUAT
The squat is how humans sit down and stand up. It's the most fundamental movement in daily life, and after 40 it's the first pattern to deteriorate — directly linked to falls, knee pain, and loss of independence. Restoring a confident squat is one of the highest-value investments you can make in your body.

🔬
40+ SCIENCE — WHY SQUAT MATTERS
Quadriceps strength is one of the strongest predictors of longevity in adults over 40. A 2022 study found that people with greater thigh muscle mass had a 32% lower risk of all-cause mortality. The squat pattern also requires significant hip mobility — and hip mobility directly impacts lower back health. Tight hip flexors from sitting create anterior pelvic tilt, a leading cause of chronic lower back pain that deep squatting progressively corrects.
💪 SQUAT EXERCISES — BEGINNER TO ADVANCED
01
Chair-Assisted Squat
BEGINNER
CHAIRBALANCE AIDCONFIDENCE BUILDER
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Stand in front of a chair, feet shoulder-width, toes pointed slightly out (10–30 degrees). Hold chair back lightly for balance — not to pull yourself up.
2
Push hips back first, then bend knees. Think: "reach back for the chair" not "bend forward at the waist."
3
Lower until you touch the chair lightly — don't collapse. Chest stays up throughout. Weight in heels and midfoot.
4
Drive through heels to stand. Squeeze glutes at the top. That's one rep.
3 SETS10–15 REPSREST 60 SEC
BAD KNEES
Use a higher chair (or add pillows) to reduce the depth of the squat. Shallower depth = significantly less knee stress. Work depth gradually over weeks.
LOWER BACK PAIN
Keep an exaggerated upright torso. If the back rounds at the bottom, the chair height is too low. Raise it.
PROGRESSION CUE
Progress to touching the chair without gripping it. Then to a box squat without a chair for guidance.
02
Box Squat
BEGINNER+
NO EQUIPMENTDEPTH CONTROLGLUTE FOCUS
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Stand with a chair or box directly behind you. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out.
2
Reach arms forward for counterbalance. Initiate by pushing hips back and sitting down as if the box will catch you.
3
Touch the box lightly and pause for one second. Don't relax. Maintain full tension throughout the pause.
4
Drive through heels. Think "push the floor away." Stand tall and lock out at the top.
3 SETS10–12 REPSREST 75 SEC
BAD KNEES
Wider stance takes load off the knees and increases hip involvement. Toes can point out more (up to 45 degrees). Use a higher box if needed.
LOWER BACK PAIN
Focus on chest staying upright. If you forward-lean, widen stance and point toes out more — this allows more upright torso without losing depth.
PROGRESSION CUE
Lower the box height by 1–2 inches every 2 weeks as strength and mobility improve. This is the safest path to a full depth squat.
03
Goblet Squat
INTERMEDIATE
DUMBBELL/KETTLEBELLCOUNTERBALANCEFULL DEPTH
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell vertically at chest height with both hands — like a goblet. This weight acts as a counterbalance that actually makes squatting deeper easier.
2
Feet slightly wider than shoulder-width, toes angled out. Elbows track down between your knees as you descend.
3
Descend until thighs are parallel or below. The weight helps keep your chest upright. At the bottom, use elbows to gently push knees out.
4
Drive through the full foot to stand. Exhale on the way up.
3 SETS8–12 REPSREST 75 SEC
BAD KNEES
This is actually one of the best squat variations for knee issues — the wide stance and heel-weighted position distributes load more favorably. Use a lighter weight and partial depth.
PROGRESSION CUE
Increase dumbbell weight by 5 lbs when 3×12 is clean. When holding 25+ lbs in goblet position, you're ready for loaded split squats.
04
Split Squat
INTERMEDIATE
UNILATERALBALANCEHIP FLEXOR
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Take a large step forward — front foot flat, back foot toes on the floor. Feet should be hip-width apart laterally (not on a tightrope).
2
Torso upright. Lower straight down until the back knee hovers just above the floor. The front shin stays vertical — don't let the knee cave in.
3
Drive through the front heel to rise. Keep 70% of weight in the front foot throughout.
4
Complete all reps on one side before switching.
3 SETS8–10 REPS EACH SIDEREST 75 SEC
BAD KNEES
Shorten your stance to reduce depth. Place back knee on a folded mat. Focus on a slow, controlled descent — pain usually comes from dropping too fast.
BALANCE ISSUES
Hold a chair or wall for balance. This is not cheating — it allows you to focus on the movement pattern without fear of falling.
PROGRESSION CUE
Add dumbbells once 3×10 bodyweight is solid. Start with 10–15 lbs per hand. This is one of the best single-leg strength builders available.
05
Bodyweight Squat
INTERMEDIATE
NO EQUIPMENTFULL DEPTHBENCHMARK
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. Arms forward or crossed at chest. Take a breath in before descending.
2
Push hips back and down simultaneously — it's not a sit-back or a sit-down, it's both at once. Knees track over toes.
3
Descend to at least parallel — thighs level with the floor. Heels must stay flat. If they rise, mobility work is needed.
4
Drive through heels to rise. Breathe out on the way up. Lock out completely at the top.
3 SETS10–15 REPSREST 60 SEC
BAD KNEES
Squat to a target — a box, a chair cushion, a yoga block. The target prevents you from going past a comfortable depth and gives confidence.
PROGRESSION CUE
The 40+ benchmark: 3×15 full depth with perfect form. When achieved, add a goblet hold or resistance band above the knees for added challenge.

MOVEMENT PATTERN 04 · POSTERIOR CHAIN
HINGE
The hip hinge is bending forward at the hips while keeping the spine neutral. It's picking something up off the floor, loading the dishwasher, tying your shoes. After 40, most people perform this movement with their lower back instead of their hips — which is the primary cause of back injuries. The hinge teaches you to load the hips and spare the spine.
GLUTESHAMSTRINGSLOWER BACK (SUPPORT)CORESPINAL ERECTORS
5

🔬
40+ SCIENCE — WHY HINGE MATTERS
Glute weakness is epidemic in sedentary adults over 40. When the glutes don't fire properly, the lower back muscles compensate — becoming chronically overworked and inflamed. Glute bridges and hip hinges directly reactivate dormant glute fibers, reducing lower back load by up to 40% in daily activities. The posterior chain — glutes, hamstrings, lower back — also contains the body's largest muscle groups and is responsible for nearly all explosive power, making it the primary driver of metabolic rate.
💪 HINGE EXERCISES — BEGINNER TO ADVANCED
01
Glute Bridge
BEGINNER
NO EQUIPMENTFLOORBACK SAFEKNEE SAFE
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart. Feet about 12 inches from your glutes.
2
Arms flat by your sides, palms down. Take a breath in. Press the lower back gently into the floor — this is your starting position.
3
Drive through heels and squeeze glutes to lift hips. At the top, your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders. Hold for 2 counts.
4
Lower with control — don't drop. The descent should take 2–3 seconds. Don't rest at the bottom — maintain tension and repeat.
3 SETS12–15 REPSREST 60 SEC
LOWER BACK PAIN
This is often the first prescribed exercise for lower back rehab. If you feel it in your back instead of your glutes, reduce range of motion and focus on the glute squeeze before lifting.
BAD KNEES
This exercise is excellent for knee conditions — it builds glute strength that directly reduces knee strain. Keep feet flat and avoid pointing toes up.
PROGRESSION CUE
When 3×15 is easy, add a 3-second hold at the top of every rep. Then progress to single-leg glute bridges.
02
Single-Leg Glute Bridge
INTERMEDIATE

▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Start in the standard glute bridge position. Extend one leg straight — toes pointing up toward the ceiling.
2

03
Hip Hinge (Bodyweight)
BEGINNER
NO EQUIPMENTSPINE TEACHINGFOUNDATION
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Stand with feet hip-width. Place one hand on lower back and one on your sternum. The goal is to feel your back stay flat throughout.
2
Soft bend in the knees — not a squat, just unlocked. Push hips backward as if reaching for a wall behind you.
3
Hinge until your torso is close to parallel with the floor. Your weight shifts to your heels. You should feel a stretch in the hamstrings.
4
Drive hips forward to stand. Squeeze glutes at the top. This is the movement you'll eventually do with weight in the Romanian Deadlift.

LOWER BACK PAIN
Stand near a wall and use it to guide the hip push-back. When you feel your hips touch the wall, that's the hinge position. This teaches the movement without risk.

04
Romanian Deadlift (Dumbbell)
INTERMEDIATE
DUMBBELLSLOADED HINGEPOSTERIOR CHAIN
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Hold dumbbells in front of thighs, palms facing you. Stand tall with chest up and shoulders packed back.
2
Hinge at hips — push them back. Dumbbells travel close to your legs (almost dragging). Knees stay soft but don't bend further.

MOVEMENT PATTERN 05 · FULL BODY
CARRY
Carry patterns are the most underrated movement in fitness. Walking while holding weight develops full-body stability, grip strength, shoulder stability, core strength, and cardiovascular fitness simultaneously. After 40, grip strength is one of the single strongest predictors of overall health and longevity — and carries build it directly.
GRIP STRENGTHTRAPEZIUSCORE ANTI-ROTATIONSHOULDER STABILIZERSENTIRE LOWER BODY

🔬
40+ SCIENCE — WHY CARRY MATTERS
A 2018 JAMA Internal Medicine study following 140,000 adults found that every 11-pound decrease in grip strength was associated with a 17% higher risk of cardiovascular death. Grip strength declines at approximately 2–3% per year after age 40 if untrained — making it one of the fastest-declining physical capacities. Farmer carries are the most efficient grip-strengthening exercise available and simultaneously challenge every stabilizing muscle in the body, making them one of the highest-value exercises per minute in existence.
💪 CARRY EXERCISES — BEGINNER TO ADVANCED
01
Suitcase Carry (Light)
BEGINNER

HOW TO PERFORM

Hold a dumbbell (or a heavy bag, grocery bag, full water jug) in one hand at your side. Stand tall — shoulder blades back and down.

PROGRESSION CUE
When you can walk 40 steps per side with zero torso tilt, increase weight by 5 lbs or extend to 60 steps per side.

CORE
The core is not your abs. It's the entire cylinder of muscles surrounding your spine — front, sides, and back. After 40, true core training means anti-movement: the ability to resist forces trying to bend, twist, or compress the spine. Crunches are not core training. Planks, dead bugs, and pallof presses are.

40+ SCIENCE — WHY CORE MATTERS
The transverse abdominis — the deepest core muscle — acts like a natural weight belt, compressing the spine to protect it during every movement. After 40, this muscle becomes inhibited due to poor posture and sedentary behavior, leaving the spine vulnerable. Research by Dr. Stuart McGill at the University of Waterloo established that anti-extension and anti-rotation core training (planks, dead bugs) is significantly more spine-protective than flexion training (sit-ups, crunches) — which can actually increase disc pressure and accelerate degeneration.

2

02
Dead Bug
BEGINNER
NO EQUIPMENTFLOORBACK SAFECOORDINATION
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Lie on your back. Arms pointing straight up to the ceiling. Knees bent at 90 degrees, shins parallel to the floor — "tabletop" position.
2
Press your lower back firmly into the floor and keep it there for the entire exercise. This is the core engagement cue.
3
Simultaneously lower your right arm overhead and extend your left leg toward the floor — both hovering 2 inches above without touching.
4
Return to start slowly. Switch sides. The movement should be slow and controlled — 3–4 seconds per extension.
3 SETS8–10 REPS EACH SIDEREST 45 SEC
LOWER BACK PAIN
This is one of the best back-safe core exercises. The key is maintaining the lower back pressing into the floor. If it lifts, your range of motion is too large. Reduce how far you extend.
COORDINATION DIFFICULTY
Isolate the movement — practice just the arm extension for a week, then just the leg extension, then combine. This is a skill as much as an exercise.
PROGRESSION CUE
Add a resistance band between hands and feet for additional anti-extension resistance. Or slow the movement to 5–6 seconds per rep.
03
Bird Dog
BEGINNER

HOW TO PERFORM
1
On hands and knees, table-top position. Wrists under shoulders, knees under hips. Spine neutral — not arched or rounded.
2

PROGRESSION CUE
Add a resistance band around the wrists and ankles for added anti-rotation demand. Or touch knee to elbow at the bottom for an advanced version.
04
Full Forearm Plank
INTERMEDIATE
NO EQUIPMENTFULL BODY TENSIONBENCHMARK
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
On forearms and toes. Elbows under shoulders. Body forms one straight line from heels to the crown of your head.
2
Engage everything simultaneously: grip the floor with your forearms, squeeze glutes, brace core, push heels back.
3
The goal is maximum full-body tension, not just holding the position. Think of "dragging your elbows toward your feet" — this activates the lats and makes the plank significantly more effective.
4
Hold. Breathe through the nose. Don't hold your breath — elevated blood pressure risk in 40+ adults during breath-holding under load.
3 SETS20–60 SEC HOLDSREST 60 SEC
HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE
Never hold your breath during planks. Continuous nasal breathing is mandatory. If holding posture causes you to hold breath involuntarily, reduce hold time to 15 seconds and build from there.
LOWER BACK PAIN
If the lower back aches, squeeze glutes harder — glute engagement is the primary protector of the lower back during planks. Also ensure the hips aren't too high or too low.
PROGRESSION CUE
The TUF 40+ benchmark: 3×60 second forearm plank with zero form breakdown. When achieved, advance to shoulder taps or plank reaches.
05
Side Plank (Modified)
INTERMEDIATE
NO EQUIPMENTANTI-LATERAL FLEXIONOBLIQUES
▼
HOW TO PERFORM
1
Lie on your side. Bottom elbow directly under shoulder. Knees bent (modified) or legs extended (advanced). Hips stacked.
2
Lift hips off the ground. Your body forms a straight diagonal line from knees (or feet) to shoulders. Top hip doesn't sag forward or backward.
3
Top arm along your body or raised to the ceiling. Breathe. Hold the position — resist the urge to let the hip sag toward the floor.
4
Hold, lower, switch sides.
3 SETS20–40 SEC EACH SIDEREST 45 SEC

Reduce hold time and ensure the supporting elbow is perfectly under the shoulder. If direct shoulder loading causes pain, skip side planks and add lateral band walks instead for oblique activation.
HIP PAIN
Place a folded mat under the supporting hip. The ground contact point can cause discomfort — padding eliminates this entirely.
PROGRESSION CUE
When 3×40 seconds modified is solid, extend to full side plank on feet. Then add hip dips (controlled) for an oblique strength challenge.
